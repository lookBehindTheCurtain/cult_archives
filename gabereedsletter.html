Living in The Book of Acts

“In the presence of God and of Christ Jesus, who will judge the living and the dead, and in view of his appearing and his kingdom, I give you this charge: Preach the word; be prepared in season and out of season; correct, rebuke and encourage—with great patience and careful instruction. For the time will come when people will not put up with sound doctrine. Instead, to suit their own desires, they will gather around them a great number of teachers to say what their itching ears want to hear. They will turn their ears away from the truth and turn aside to myths. But you, keep your head in all situations, endure hardship, do the work of an evangelist, discharge all the duties of your ministry.”
 2 Timothy 4:1-5

I wasn’t going to write anything until later on down the road. On our way out, my wife and I really just wanted to focus on moving forward with God and with our lives. But the day we left, our email was flooded with people who said they were feeling like they wanted to leave as well. Even still, I didn’t want to persuade anyone one way or another. But then I remembered the Scripture above. This was the Scripture that my father in the faith read to me when he appointed me as an Evangelist at the 2014 Global Leadership Conference.

In front of 2,000 witnesses, I made a declaration to uphold this Scripture—to preach the word, to correct, rebuke, and encourage. Because the day will come, and I believe has come, when men will not put up with sound doctrine but instead gather around a great number of teachers to say what their itching ears want to hear. And then I remembered that by that oath, I am obligated to speak up, speak out, and speak against any doctrine that is not sound. I do not claim to be a Bible scholar, but that word in the Greek is hygiano , which means “to be sound, to be well, to be in good health.”

If you want to save yourself time reading through this rather long document, then know that essentially we left the International Christian Churches/SoldOut Discipling Movement because we found the doctrine to not be sound or healthy for true Christians. You can be saved there, but I believe eventually you have to leave because the foundation of the movement is broken. We went all the way back to when we first studied the First Principles and decided to be noble Bereans (Acts 17:10-12) and check up on what our religious leaders have told us to see if what they were saying was true. We found more deviations from the Scriptures than we expected, and so we made the decision that God was calling us to come out of the ICC.

Why Did We Leave?

Anyone and everyone in the ICC would agree that, from time to time, decisions are made that just don’t sit well with you. A pledge drive, an extra push for Special Missions, a young leader in your church being sent somewhere, or being asked/told to do something you don’t really want to do. I’m sure we could come up with a long list of things. Most of the time, we say “Amen” and get behind whatever is being done, chalking it up to “that’s just how things are in the kingdom,” “this is the need of the hour,” or “the movement is moving,” or whatever expression gets you through it. After seven and a half years of saying “Amen,” I finally decided to investigate and search the Scriptures as to why we do what we do. I felt obligated to do so because of the oath I made as an Evangelist, to ensure that the doctrine is sound.

Also, I noticed things happening differently than when I first got baptized. Things were changing, and something inside told me it wasn’t for the better. The reality is that I came to the conviction to leave the ICC because I studied the doctrine of the ICC and found it not to be sound doctrine. I did not consult anyone from the ICOC, nor did I talk to someone who was marked or disfellowshipped until after I made the decision to leave. I read our official church documents: First Principles Bible Study Series, Revolution Through Restoration I, II, III, The Biblical Differences Between The ICOC and the ICC, A Concern For All The Churches,  and kipmckean.com .

After reading our official documents, I came to the conclusion that the reason why we feel like something is off from time to time is because something is  off—it’s the foundation on which the movement is built. Today in the ICC, there are at least three foundational issues (I believe there are many more, but these were enough for me to make the decision to leave):

  1. The Heretical Implementation of the Five Core Convictions
  2. The Corporate Structure of the Movement Leadership That Makes Sinful Leadership Normal
  3. A Movement Built on Money

The Heretical Implementation of the Movement’s Five Core Conviction

Let me start by saying that I absolutely agree with 3 of the 5 Core Convictions. One of them is an opinion and a nice goal so I agree with that one too and one is just down right heretical. However, what I firmly disagree with is how we implement all five of these convictions.

A Bible Church v. New Testament Church

Taken from your writing in the article “The Biblical Differences Between the International Church of Christ and the International Christian Churches” , Kip wrote:

“The Mainline Church of Christ is an off-shoot of the Restoration Movement begun in the United States in the early 1800’s by Thomas Campbell, Alexander Campbell, and Barton Stone, to name a few. Their plea was to “restore the New Testament Church.” Therefore, they believed that though the Old Testament was divinely inspired and historically accurate, only the New Testament would be their sole rule of faith and practice in deciding matters of doctrine, including ecclesiastical structure. This is now the conviction of the most influential leaders in the ICOC.

However, we in the ICC believe like Paul, who wrote in late 66 AD – some 35 years after the beginning of the church, “All Scripture is inspired by God and is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting, and training in righteousness.”  ( 2 Timothy 3:16 ) Technically speaking, the word “Scripture” in this passage refers only to the Old Testament. Now, through the inspiration of the Spirit, we believe that it applies as well to the New Testament. Though we believe “the Law” is no longer binding ( Colossians 2:13-14 ), the Scriptural concepts in the Old Testament, such as “calling out the remnant,” “dating and marrying only disciples,” and “a central leader and leadership for God’s movement” are in the New Testament. Yet, the Old Testament is much richer in its depth on these vital issues. Also, the “Jethro Principle” of leadership in Exodus 18  allows us to lead and take care of thousands of God’s precious people. Therefore, we believe we are commanded by God to build congregations based on both the Old and New Testaments – a “Bible Church,” not simply a “New Testament Church.”

As I read through this again and again, initially in an effort to defend our cause, I couldn’t help but ask, what did Thomas Campbell, Alexander Campbell, and Barton Stone mean when their plea was to “restore the New Testament Church”? Are they not just trying to get back to the church of the Bible? Kip often preaches regarding the evangelization of nations in this generation, “that if they did it in the first century, we can do it in the 21st century!”  Is that not trying to restore the New Testament church?

Even in Kip’s own Revolution Through Restoration  (which every ICCM student is required to read), the article says:

“During my years at Charleston and Memphis, I devoted myself to studying the Old Testament. At this major turning point in my life, I came to a deep conviction that, unlike the traditional Church of Christ which claimed only to be a ‘New Testament church,’  a better understanding of God's eternal plan would cause us to be a Bible church .”
— Revolution Through Restoration I

“The Lord allowed me to begin the restoration of the New Testament church from a small group of 30 would-be disciples in the Gempels' living room in June of 1979 in Boston.”
— Revolution Through Restoration II

So here we have three articles written by the same person in three different time periods. In the first ( Revolution Through Restoration I ), it is stated that it is better to be a Bible church rather than a New Testament church. In the second ( Revolution Through Restoration II ), the focus seems to be the restoration of the New Testament church. And in the third ( Biblical Differences Between the ICOC and ICC ), the ICOC is condemned for being just a New Testament church. It seemed to me that whichever approach was convenient to our brother Kip at the time was considered the right way.

In the article on the Biblical Differences Between the ICOC and ICC,  it was said that the ICOC’s fault was that they used only the New Testament for “deciding matters of doctrine including ecclesiastical structure.” Then 2 Timothy 3:16-17  was quoted. However, 2 Timothy 3:16-17  has nothing to do with deciding matters of doctrine as it pertains to ecclesiastical structure; it concerns teaching, correcting, rebuking, and training, primarily through preaching to prevent people from turning to false doctrine ( 2 Timothy 4:1-3 ). Why aren’t we letting the Bible interpret the Bible instead of using it to support our views?

How can we say that we are no longer bound by the Law but then threaten church discipline on anyone who doesn’t give a tithe? Tithing is an Old Testament commandment. How can we say we aren’t bound by the Law yet use Scriptures like Deuteronomy 17:12-13  to disfellowship anyone who shows contempt? It seems to me that we are binding people to the Law. The reason we rely so heavily on the Old Testament is because, without it, we cannot implement the false doctrine that there must be a centralized leadership with a central leader inside of Christianity.

I have no problem with using the Old Testament for teaching, rebuking, correcting, and training of men’s and women’s characters, as 2 Timothy 3:16-17  instructs us, but using the Old Testament to establish ecclesiastical structure is not healthy doctrine. Yes, I believe in being a Bible church, but not in the way the ICC interprets a Bible church.

In the Movement, this “Core Conviction” is implemented in such a way that all it does is bind God’s people to man-made traditions of discipleship partners (taken from Exodus 18 ), a central leader (taken from Numbers 27:15-17 ), and financial sacrifice ( Malachi 3:6-12 ). I do not think that discipleship partners, a central leader, and giving a weekly contribution are wrong. But when we don’t allow people the freedom to decide for themselves the degree to which they will implement these man-made traditions in their own lives, I believe we take liberties the Scriptures do not give us.

When we tell God’s people who is going to disciple them, that they must have a discipler because that is how we practice discipleship in our fellowship, that they must submit to the Central Leadership, and that they are required to give a contribution—all of these expectations placed upon them before they can be baptized in our fellowship—we are no better than the Pharisees. “Woe to you, teachers of the law and Pharisees, you hypocrites! You shut the door of the kingdom of heaven in people’s faces. You yourselves do not enter, nor will you let those enter who are trying to.”  ( Matthew 23:13-14 )

There is no way, before God, that we can tell people they must believe in a Bible Church as we interpret it before they can be baptized in our fellowship and think we are doing a service to the lost or to God.

Interpretation of Scripture

Another quote from your Revolution Through Restoration  article:

“Along with this, I came to differ with the Churches of Christ whose creed is 'to speak where the Bible speaks and be silent where the Bible is silent.' This creed dictated that one must have specific authorization by command, example, or necessary inference from the Bible to do anything. It developed a legalistic, Pharisaic mindset that sowed the seed for much dissension and division, producing the factions of the Churches of Christ.

For example, the whole mandate to only 'call Bible things by Bible names' is contradictory because the word 'Bible' is not even in the Bible! From the Scriptures, I came to believe the opposite. I believe that we should be silent where the Bible speaks and speak where the Bible is silent. In other words, a Christian should simply obey where the Bible speaks and only speak (have opinions) where the Bible is silent.

In building a life, a church, or a 'system' for a movement, we are 'free' to do anything the Scriptures do not specifically, by command, by example, or by necessary inference prohibit ( Colossians 2:6-23 ). The truth seeker will always strive to be faithful to the direction and the spirit of the Scriptures.”

Again, I fully believe in this conviction, but the implementation of this conviction is why we are leaving. What is actually happening is that we are not free to choose anything ; Kip and those Kip appoints hold all the authority to speak where the Bible is silent. Whenever Kip or Kip’s personally appointed leaders form an opinion on something, the rest of us are bound to it.

For instance, it was decided that we would have a three-times Missions contribution in February. I’m pretty sure the Bible is silent on how, when, and how much we are to collect for planting churches, yet someone spoke where the Bible was silent, and now we are all bound to it. Last year, someone decided how much the Global Leadership Conference would cost. No problem. But then every evangelist was hounded with weekly, sometimes daily, emails asking for an update on how many people were registered so that we would break even in the budget. Again, someone spoke where the Bible was silent and then made us  obey it and carry it out as if it is actual church doctrine.

When do I get to speak where the Bible is silent? In the movement, I don’t.

You must be joking, Raul! While doctrinal concepts were certainly a part of the discussion, it was not the main problem. Please note as well that many of the examples of “unbiblical” practices you list are still practiced today in the ICOC. Obviously, most of our churches have Bible Talks and Discipleship Partners. You need to go back and get your facts straight.”

Someone is lying. I’ve always been taught that the ICOC believes in a different interpretation of Scripture. Yet a “Lead Evangelist” from the ICOC says differently. It seems to me that we were told that the ICOC believed something different so that all the remnant disciples would stay in our movement and not go to the ICOC. I’ve never set foot in the ICOC since becoming a disciple, but I know many in the movement who have. Not one of them has ever come back and said that they believe in a different mode of interpretation of Scripture.

This Scripture says that in Christ, I’ve been brought to completeness. It says He is the head over every power and authority. So why do I need a middleman between me and Christ speaking for me where Jesus did not speak? Shouldn’t I, through prayer, fasting, Bible study, and advice, come to my own conclusion about how I should live my life? If I don’t do that, I may run the danger of someone taking me captive through hollow and deceptive philosophy (like the ICOC’s Biblical interpretations), which depends on human traditions such as World Sector Leaders, Overseeing Evangelists, and Discipleship Partners. All these are good and healthy if implemented in the right way, but we do not implement them in a healthy way, and this is why so many are leaving the movement even as I write this letter.

I’m sorry, but it seems that the plan of God is directly opposed to the Word of God. Therefore, I must conclude that this is not God’s plan and not God’s movement. God’s leader would not purposely contradict God’s Word over and over again, even after many in this movement and prior have warned him about this type of behavior.

Discipling is a Command, Not Optional

I agree 100%. We all need the fellowship of brothers and sisters who help us stay on the straight and narrow. But the implementation of this is nothing more than a way to control the flock. From your Biblical Differences  article:

“In our modern world with such busy schedules in such an individualistic and highly structured society, we have found that the only way to guarantee that every member in our congregations is being discipled is to have structured discipling—discipleship partners.”

From this, I understand that Kip looked at the structure of today’s modern world and concluded that the only way to guarantee every member in our congregation is being discipled is to have structured discipling. So, instead of looking at the Scriptures to understand the culture in God’s kingdom and imitating what we see in the Bible, he looked at the world and said, “The world is highly structured; therefore, the kingdom needs to be highly structured.” I don’t think I need to go into details as to why this is bad theology (but I will later).

The problem is that it creates an us vs. them  mentality with other disciples around the world. We automatically assume that if a group does not have a structured discipling plan, then that group does not believe in discipling. It’s a shallow way to judge whether someone is a true disciple. Someone could agree to have a discipler yet not genuinely be a disciple. This approach doesn’t address the root of the issue. If someone is not seeking advice, input, direction, correction, teaching, training, and rebuking, perhaps this person does not care about holiness or actually being a Christian.

If looking at the Bible is not convincing enough, let’s just look at the facts: discipleship partners have failed and are failing. Look at the fall-away rate around the movement—it’s close to 80% now. Even in LA, there’s a near 90% fall-away rate (369 people were baptized, and 337 people fell away in LA in 2015). So if it clearly is not working and did not work in the prior movement (since, according to Kip, this was one of the first things abandoned when the movement fell apart), then why do we keep doing it? The answer is simple: it is the best way for leadership to spread indoctrination through the fellowship.

How many times are we told as leaders that we need to discuss the 5 Core Convictions, the Crown of Thorns Project, Special Missions, the GLC, or who we need to delete from social media because they were marked or disfellowshipped, during d-times? Is this really what needs to be addressed in our personal d-times? What about holiness, righteousness, faith, hope, love, and other topics that Christ actually emphasized? We don’t do discipleship partners because they work to keep people faithful; they work because they keep people indoctrinated with our unique theology.

A Central Leadership with a Central Leader vs. Autonomous Congregations

“Throughout the Bible, the Israelites were at their strongest with the Lord when they had a central leader: Moses, Joshua, Samuel, David. As a matter of fact, the book of Judges says of those days when they did not, 'Israel had no king; everyone did as he saw fit.' ( Judges 21:25 ) In the New Testament, Jesus is the leader of 'the Movement!' Uniquely, when He ascends to Heaven, Peter takes on this responsibility as 'the apostle to the Jews,' since for the first seven years of Christianity, only Jews became Christians. Interestingly, after Paul became 'the apostle to the Gentiles,' the leadership of the movement by Acts 15  had passed to Jesus’ oldest half-brother, James. At the Jerusalem Council, James, after listening to both sides of the circumcision issue, gives his singular authoritative 'judgment,' which is then bound on all the churches ( Acts 15:19-24 ). Even Paul, after his missionary journeys, reports to James and submits to his direction ( Acts 21:24 ).

The Mainline Church of Christ and the ICOC do not believe in an authoritative central leader or leadership. Sadly, autonomous churches only produce autonomous disciples. In fact, in 2005, some of the most influential ICOC leaders came to me and begged me to be on a 'team of leaders' if I would only say that autonomy was 'a matter of opinion.' I said, 'No. It is sin.' Since I disagreed with them, I was labeled 'divisive,' which is what Jesus understood would happen if you preach the truth ( Luke 12:51-53 ).”

As you said earlier in this article, the Old Testament is richer in examples of centralized leadership with a central leader than the New Testament. That’s because the Old Testament deals with the physical nation of Israel. The Israelites weren’t just the people of God; they were an independent state. The centralized leadership of the Old Testament was not only to help the Israelites stay close to God but also to enforce the laws of their nation. There are many things that distinguish the Old Covenant from the New Covenant, but I want to focus on one: The Holy Spirit.

“I will sprinkle clean water on you, and you will be clean; I will cleanse you from all your impurities and from all your idols. I will give you a new heart and put a new spirit in you; I will remove from you your heart of stone and give you a heart of flesh. And I will put my Spirit in you and move you to follow my decrees and be careful to keep my laws.”
 Ezekiel 36:25-27

“And I will ask the Father, and he will give you another advocate to help you and be with you forever—the Spirit of truth. The world cannot accept him, because it neither sees him nor knows him. But you know him, for he lives with you and will be in you.”
 John 14:16-17

Local Autonomy in the First Century Church

In the first-century church, there was local autonomy, yet all the churches still worked together. For instance, Jerusalem had no part in Paul and Barnabas being sent out on a missionary journey from Antioch. Paul didn’t go to Iconium, Lystra, Cyprus, and Derbe under the direction of the apostles in Jerusalem. The “Jerusalem” of Our Movement vs. the Jerusalem in Acts

At this point, I want to highlight a significant difference: the “Jerusalem” of our movement and the Jerusalem in the book of Acts. In our movement, LA is considered our “Jerusalem” based on Acts 1:8 . LA has the most members, the biggest staff, and the largest contribution. Eventually, many of our most talented young and veteran disciples are sent to LA to be trained because “Jerusalem” is meant to be a model for the other churches. Additionally, in our movement, our “Jerusalem” makes all the decisions for the rest of the churches.

This is far from the Jerusalem of Scripture. The Jerusalem in the Bible was a poor church—not the one with the biggest payroll. Jerusalem was so poor that the disciples weren’t even eating, let alone having enough money to pay all its leaders ( Acts 6:1 ). Furthermore, the apostles didn’t make all the decisions for Jerusalem. When the Hellenistic Jews were overlooked in the distribution of food, the apostles didn’t choose the seven men to handle it; the disciples themselves chose the seven and presented them to the apostles ( Acts 6:1-6 ).

While this may seem minor, when the Scriptures are retold inaccurately, our modern-day apostles (Central Leadership) retain all the power within their hands. The Jerusalem church didn’t oversee the planting of every church in the book of Acts like our modern-day “Jerusalem” does. If you read the Scriptures closely, you’ll find that, many times, the apostles didn’t even know a church had started. Look at Philip in Samaria ( Acts 8:14 ), Barnabas explaining to the apostles about Paul’s work in Damascus ( Acts 9:26-28 ), and Paul explaining to the apostles about the churches planted among the Gentiles ( Acts 15:4 ). Often, the apostles had no idea these things were happening.

We’re often told that Jerusalem was the “center” of the movement because of the council in Acts 15.  But the reason the council took place in Jerusalem was because the false doctrine had originated there ( Acts 15:1 ). This wasn’t a gathering of every church leader in the movement; it was a council of the apostles and elders from Jerusalem and Antioch, and that’s it. The letter was sent to the other churches due to Antioch’s influence on those churches, not Jerusalem’s. Paul took the letter back to the churches he planted—churches that Jerusalem did not plant.

If we were truly “Living in the Book of Acts,” we wouldn’t be under the constant direction of our modern-day “Jerusalem.” But I digress…

Local Autonomy and the Diversity of the Disciples

Another example of local autonomy is the diversity of teachings among the disciples. In Jerusalem, disciples were taught to keep the law as Christians, while Paul taught people that they didn’t need to keep the law ( Acts 21:20-22 ). We’ve incorrectly taught that all disciples were taught the same thing everywhere, in every church ( 1 Corinthians 4:17 ). But the Scriptures indicate that Paul taught the same thing everywhere he went—not that every preacher taught the same thing everywhere. For example, there were disciples of John who preached about the Messiah but knew nothing about the Holy Spirit ( Acts 19:1-5 ), and there were preachers who spoke about Jesus but didn’t know Paul’s teachings ( Acts 18:24-28 ).

It’s clear that the Holy Spirit—not the apostles in Jerusalem—sent Philip to preach in Azotus ( Acts 8:39-40 ). Peter was directed by the Holy Spirit to go to Joppa and convert the first Gentile ( Acts 10:9-48 ). These are examples of people preaching without direction from a central leadership. We would call this autonomy, and it’s certainly not sinful. This is simply preaching the word. Just because an overseeing evangelist doesn’t instruct you to do something doesn’t mean you’re selfish or trying to build your own kingdom.

How Movements Succeed Without Centralized Leadership

But how can we expect to succeed as a movement without Centralized Leadership? The same way every other movement has survived from the beginning of time: through respect and cooperation. The First Century church did not have a Central Leader (though some in our Central Leadership argue James was a leader, he wasn’t making decisions for every church globally because he didn’t know about every church globally).

The Civil Rights Movement did not have Centralized Leadership with a single leader making decisions for every minority across America. Nor did the Women’s Suffrage Movement, the Reformation Movement, or the American Revolution. These movements had key contributors but no defined leader who made decisions for every member of their movement.

This is why our movement resembles the Catholic Church. In Catholicism, the Pope makes all decisions for the Church. He defines doctrine and interprets Scripture, with his Central Leadership responsible for advising him and executing his decisions—all with the goal of keeping Catholics, Catholic. Similarly, all decisions our Central Leader makes are intended to keep members of the movement in the movement. Sadly, though, our movement has become a church that turned into a business—and is now just a business trying to retain the properties of a church.

A Movement Built on Money

I want to start with a clear statement so there’s no confusion about what I believe: I believe in Special Missions, and I believe we should take up a Special Missions contribution each year. How Special Missions Contributions Really Work

Currently, this is how Special Missions operates: The Crown of Thorns Council—which includes our World Sector Leaders, movement Shepherds, administrators, and a select group of Evangelists and Women’s Ministry Leaders—gathers to assess the state of our movement and make plans for its future. Based on their judgment, they determine the multiplier for Special Missions and how many times we’ll hold Special Missions in a year. In the 18 months I’ve led a church, we’ve had three Special Missions, with another three scheduled for 2016.

The amount each church is expected to contribute in the Spring is typically a multiple of the previous October’s weekly contribution average. Fall Missions are based on the June contribution average, which is why pledge drives are often scheduled in January and after the GLC—to ensure we “blow out” our Special Missions goal based on new pledges. As a church leader, my only flexibility is in correcting any misreported averages during these periods, as we’re required to send monthly reports to LA. However, I have no say in the total multiple we raise or send; we are expected to send everything except a 1x to cover our weekly needs. This model presents a significant Biblical issue.

The Biblical Issue with Our Special Missions Model

The issue is threefold:

  1. The first-century church didn’t operate this way.  Special Missions as we know it doesn’t exist in the Bible, and while we may reference Old Testament examples (e.g., collecting funds for the temple or tabernacle), these were one-time collections, not annual ones. Pointing to David’s example or similar stories for annual fundraising doesn’t align with New Testament practices.
  2. Claiming we are imitating the first-century church is misleading.  If we were following their model, we wouldn’t command disciples on what to give each year. When David collected funds for the temple, the people gave willingly without being told how much they were expected to contribute ( 2 Chronicles 29:6 ). In the New Covenant and the book of Acts, we don’t see this annual demand for specific amounts.
  3. The current model leads people to “pay” rather than “give” to Special Missions.  No matter how well-intentioned the leaders or disciples may be, the structure promotes giving under compulsion rather than generosity.

Misinterpreted Scriptures on Collections and Contributions

For anyone familiar with the Bible, several Scriptures likely come to mind regarding taking up collections and sending money back to Jerusalem. These passages are often used to justify our model of Special Missions: Acts 4:32-36; Romans 15:25-28; 1 Corinthians 16:1-4; 2 Corinthians 8-9;  and Acts 11:27-30 . On the surface, these seem to support the idea of collecting funds and sending them to LA for our central leadership to distribute. However, the one major flaw in this interpretation is that the money Paul collected was for benevolence , not missions work . In the first century, the apostles never collected funds specifically for planting churches.

Instead, the apostles were focused on meeting the needs of local congregations, especially in the distribution of food ( Acts 6:1-7; 11:27-30 ). Jerusalem went through a famine, leaving many disciples without food. Paul collected funds to support the poor church in Jerusalem—a church quite different from our modern-day “Jerusalem” in LA, which has the highest weekly contribution (25x that of Eugene). In all these cited Scriptures, the collection was for benevolent purposes, not church planting. Although we might say that “we are materially rich here in America and need to support the spiritually poor worldwide through missions,” we cannot use these Scriptures to justify that view.

The Importance of Differentiating Benevolence from Missions

The Scriptures and the practice of the first-century church show that nearly all collected funds were for benevolence, not planting churches. Why is this distinction important? Because if a leader uses these Scriptures to demand a specific multiple of weekly contributions for Missions, they are no longer imitating the New Testament. Paul explicitly states that disciples gave what they decided in their hearts, not what others told them to give ( Acts 11:29; 2 Corinthians 9:7 ).

The reason disciples raised so much for benevolence was that Jesus taught it was a salvation issue to care for the poor and needy, especially among believers ( Matthew 25:31-46 ). When Paul spoke about “testing the sincerity of your faith,” he referred to willingness to support brothers and sisters in need, not mission contributions. Some might argue that supporting missionaries in challenging countries is a priority, and I agree. But such support would be a benevolent offering, not a mandatory multiple of weekly contributions through Missions. Misunderstanding or misrepresenting this distinction risks misappropriating funds, which is not only unethical but illegal.

Sadly, in this respect, we aren’t much different from other churches that allocate only 5-10% of their donations to local benevolence. Within our movement, most churches allocate about 10% to local benevolence. I exclude MERCY Worldwide payments here since local churches are required to give to MERCY Worldwide, meaning it’s not strictly benevolent.

Benevolence and Church Planting in the First Century

Another reason why the disciples could focus on giving benevolence is because, in the first century, they did not need money to plant churches. I know this doesn’t seem possible, but if you read the book of Acts, that’s exactly what happened.

So, how were churches planted? Often, it was through persecution. The starting of new churches in other cities came as a result of having to relocate due to persecution. (Acts 8:1; 14:1-6)

Another way churches were planted in Acts was that those planting churches funded their missions personally. For instance, when Paul arrived in a city, he often worked to support himself. (1 Corinthians 18:1-4; 1 Thessalonians 2:9-10)

Wouldn’t this be a cheaper solution than raising millions to plant a few churches a year? In 2015, according to a bulletin published by the City of Angels church, we raised $2.6 million and planted 6 churches. I know for a fact that it doesn’t cost $433,000 to start a church. This means that even though we contributed significantly in 2014 for missions in places like Sydney, Australia, we continued to fundraise to support them in 2015. In fact, we’re supporting churches in North America, South America, Africa, and Europe year after year.

Despite planting more churches, the financial burden on members has increased to support previously funded churches. (It may seem confusing, but Central Leadership's approach each year enables this ongoing financial expectation from members.)

Paying Special Missions Contributions

The final issue with Missions is that we don’t “give” Special Missions contributions—we “pay” them. Central Leadership doesn’t merely suggest that we contribute 20x in May; they expect it, often spending Missions funds before the full amount is even collected.

Typically, a genuine gift is unexpected and freely given. For instance, in the Book of Acts, people gave based on their ability (Acts 11:29-30) . However, if we are told how much to contribute, it becomes an instruction rather than a gift. With Special Missions, we’re informed of the amount and timeline, so it’s a compulsion rather than a willing contribution (2 Corinthians 9:7) .

Central Leadership circumvents direct requests by only setting expectations for the entire church to meet specific financial goals. So, while one person may give 3x and another 37x, the overall goal is what matters to leadership. By placing the pressure on churches through inspiring sermons, stories, and impact emails, they avoid “technically” compelling any individual to give.

Yet, if the Bible prohibits dictating individual giving amounts, how can one church enforce this on another church? My movement-minded leaders might cite Paul’s instructions for the churches to support Jerusalem, but this was benevolence, not a set mission goal.

If we truly aim to model the Book of Acts, Central Leadership should collect offerings responsibly rather than imposing predetermined missions contributions. Forcing Special Missions contributions every three months, as we currently do (9x in November, 3x in February, and 20x in May), is backward. As the saying goes, “We shouldn’t put the cart before the horse.”

Closing Thoughts and Proposed Solutions for Missions Giving

To close out, I want to reiterate my opening statement: I believe in Special Missions, and I believe we should take up a Special Missions contribution every year.  If you're looking for support for why we should not take Special Missions, this is not the article. We absolutely need to support World Missions and be passionate about it. I think it is very beneficial for us “rich” American disciples to sacrifice for those on the Mission field in less fortunate areas.

However, our current model is totally unbiblical and doesn’t appear to be blessed by God  if we look beyond the raw data. For example, raising $2.6 million and planting only 6 churches hardly seems like a blessing from God.

Every church in the world believes that every good thing that happens in their church is a blessing from God, but often these religious communities are as misguided as their leaders on doctrines of salvation. We need to test the Spirit and consider our ways if we truly want to evangelize the world in this generation.

Solution 1: Allow Local Evangelists More Control Over Special Missions Goals

One solution is for the Local Evangelist to establish a Special Missions goal for their own church  and decide how much stays in the local church versus how much is sent out. Currently, our Evangelists function more like glorified supervisors; unless recognized in the Crown Thorn Council, they have little say in church planting or fundraising goals.

Why not let Evangelists and Shepherds operate in their intended roles, instead of as Fundraising Coordinators? Shouldn’t local leaders be able to decide, based on the needs of their church and the movement, what their own Special Missions goal should be?

If our Evangelists are not capable of making such a spiritually informed decision, we have a larger issue than simply not raising enough money this year.

Solution 2: Invest in Existing Churches Rather Than Constant Expansion

Another solution is to stop having churches that aren’t growing plant new churches . According to the City of Angels financial presentation, our fifth fastest-growing church in 2015 was only growing at 41%, with the remaining 56 churches growing at less than 40%, and some even showing negative growth.

Wouldn’t it make more sense to invest in growing our existing churches instead of pressuring struggling ones to sacrifice even more? For example, the Eugene church went from 56 disciples to 46 in 2015, even after sending out 8 Bible Talk Leaders to support other churches and beginning the New Year by sending out 3 more leaders.

Why not focus on sustaining what we have before expanding ? Currently, we raise money to plant new churches but then have to fund them indefinitely, sending our best leaders to LA for training while our local churches struggle.

Solution 3: Prioritize Planting More American Churches

Why not focus on cheaper church plantings within the United States ? Planting in American cities would be more affordable and would expand our base of contributors for Special Missions.

For example, Gainesville was planted for only $15,000, and Sacramento for $23,000. Compare this to the cost of planting in expensive cities like Dubai or in third-world countries, where financial support is required indefinitely. While we are not more important than other nations, maximizing our resources would prevent the exhaustion of funds before they’re raised.

Solution 4: Live Out the Book of Acts by Minimizing Full-Time Salaries

A more radical solution, but one I’m willing to be the first to try, is to live in the Book of Acts  by minimizing full-time salaries for church leaders. In the Book of Acts, most church planting was done without full-time salaries.

Our movement’s push to get as many people as possible into full-time ministry increases costs. I’ve already documented instances where Paul preached full-time without pay. While Paul states, “Those who preach the gospel should receive their living from the gospel” (1 Corinthians 9:14) , he also emphasizes that he doesn’t want to be paid if it would hinder the gospel (1 Corinthians 9:12) .

If we’re forcing members to give 28x their weekly contribution, which equates to nearly three weeks’ salary for a 10% tithe or over a month’s salary for those giving 16%, we’re hindering spiritual growth . Perhaps this is one reason our movement has a 50% fall-away rate.

Why not plant churches without money, as they did in the Book of Acts? We could work regular jobs, stop renting expensive conference centers, and hold workshops and retreats in homes instead. Expensive conferences can be a burden, especially when disciples back home are struggling to pay rent.

Let’s truly live in the Book of Acts!
